## Context

A Freie Universität Berlin PhD researcher was building [PrivacyRisq](https://privacyrisq.f4.htw-berlin.de/), converting real privacy incidents into measurable insights. She had the data and was deep in the core research problem.

What was missing: a product perspective.

My first questions: *Who actually uses this? In what situation do they open it? How do we make this data mean something and not just display it?*

I joined with zero background in privacy compliance, GDPR, or threat modeling.

## What I Did

**Set the direction** — Ramped up on LINDDUN, GDPR, MITRE ATT&CK. Defined goals and delivrables with the researcher and set up our working rhythm.

**Structured the analysis** — Mapped the dimensions the data could be analyzed from. Agreed priorities with the researcher. Key decision: don't just show charts but use a narrative structure so the analysis actually lands.

**Designed the product** — User analysis → MVP scope → product design. Structure, information hierarchy, UI preference and interaction flow.

**Built and shipped** — AI pair coding. Full data pipeline, visualizations, and MVP in 3 days. Delivered during exam period.

## The Product

The full design covers landing page, dashboard, and case library.
Merve already had the landing page — I focused on the two analytical surfaces.

### Dashboard — macro insights

Built for researchers who need to read the data fast and correctly.
Narrative structure + deliberate chart selection + intentional color use — each choice made to transmit information efficiently and without introducing bias.

One thing I wanted but didn't ship due to time: inline methodology notes. The idea was that whenever a user questions a number or a framing, the explanation is already there — no context-switching. That's on the backlog.

### Case Library — granular research

Built for researchers who need to go deep on specific incidents.
Two entry points: curated cases and the full dataset. Flexible filtering — jurisdiction, LINDDUN category, threat actor, data source — so users can find exactly what they're looking for. The design work here was mostly about information architecture: what to show, in what order, and how much.
