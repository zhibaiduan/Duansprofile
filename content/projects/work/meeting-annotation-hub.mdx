{/* PROBLEM */}
<div className="section">
  <div className="section-label">The Problem</div>
  <h2>Strategic decisions were made in meetings.<br />Most of it got lost before execution.</h2>

  <p className="prose">NetDragon is a 3,000-person HK-listed EdTech and gaming company. The CEO held daily meetings with senior leadership and project leads. These were <strong>not routine syncs</strong>. They were <strong>high-level strategic sessions</strong> where company direction, key decisions, and project initiations happened.</p>

  <p className="prose">Two problems came up repeatedly.</p>

  <div className="problem-grid">
    <div className="problem-cell">
      <span className="p-icon">🧠</span>
      <h3>No place to revisit thinking</h3>
      <p>The CEO generated ideas and direction in these sessions. Once the meeting ended, there was no way to go back, reflect, or build on what was said.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="problem-cell">
      <span className="p-icon">⚠️</span>
      <h3>Decisions drifted during execution</h3>
      <p>By the time decisions reached the people doing the work, the original reasoning was gone. They were acting on summaries, not the real intent.</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <p className="prose">The CEO's fix: require key participants to <strong>annotate meetings after the fact</strong>. Not just read a summary. Actually re-engage with the content and write their own understanding. The annotation itself was the mechanism to force deeper processing.</p>
</div>

{/* SITUATION I INHERITED */}
<div className="section">
  <div className="section-label">What I Inherited</div>
  <h2>A tool that existed.<br />Almost nobody was using it.</h2>

  <p className="prose">A meeting annotation tool was already in place. It supported text annotation, handwriting input, and approval workflows. Very complete. Almost completely avoided.</p>

  <p className="prose">The core problem was <strong>speed</strong>. Opening a meeting was slow. Writing an annotation was slow. Teams who had to use it complained. Teams who didn't, stopped trying.</p>

  <p className="prose">The previous owner had left. I assessed the technical debt with the dev team. Our conclusion: <strong>rebuilding from scratch was faster and cleaner than fixing what existed</strong>. So that is what we did.</p>

  <p className="prose">I was a junior product designer at the time. This was my first role as both product owner and project lead, with a team of 3.5, reporting to a senior product director and the CEO.</p>
</div>

{/* WHAT I BUILT */}
<div className="section">
  <div className="section-label">What I Built</div>
  <h2>Three stages of product evolution.</h2>

  <div className="journey">

    <div className="journey-step">
      <div className="step-dot">1</div>
      <div className="step-body">
        <h4>⚡ From broken tool to usable core</h4>
        <p>I started with business research. Talked to the teams who were supposed to use the tool. The problem was clear: too slow, too complex, too many features nobody needed.</p>
        <p>The MVP cut everything non-essential. <strong>Two things only: meeting load speed and annotation efficiency.</strong> Nothing else shipped until those felt right.</p>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div className="journey-step">
      <div className="step-dot">2</div>
      <div className="step-body">
        <h4>🔔 From annotation tool to collaboration tool</h4>
        <p>As more people started using it, I noticed a pattern. People were sharing meeting transcripts in chat to give context to colleagues. The content had value. The way it moved around was just very manual.</p>
        <p>That observation became a feature set: @ mentions, task dispatch, annotation reactions, and data sync. <strong>Collaboration was not planned from the start. It was discovered.</strong></p>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div className="journey-step">
      <div className="step-dot">3</div>
      <div className="step-body">
        <h4>🗂️ From collaboration tool to org knowledge tool</h4>
        <p>Annotations were piling up. I noticed something in myself: I enjoyed browsing senior leaders' annotations. It helped me understand project direction and learn how decisions were being made.</p>
        <p><strong>That personal habit became a product decision.</strong> I built a page that surfaced all annotations as topic-tagged cards with links to the original transcript. Users then asked for subscriptions so updates could come to them instead of them going looking.</p>
      </div>
    </div>

  </div>
</div>
