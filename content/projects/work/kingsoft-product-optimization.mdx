<div className="section">
  <div className="section-label">Background</div>
  <h2>Growth in a mature product had to come from depth, not noise.</h2>

  <p className="prose">Kingsoft&apos;s online spreadsheet was already a national-scale product with more than 10M active users. It was feature-complete, well established, and highly visible inside the company. I joined without a predefined brief, so the first question I had to answer was strategic rather than tactical: where can a product this mature still create meaningful progress?</p>

  <p className="prose">The team&apos;s core tracking metrics were daily active users, monthly active users, and active file counts. At this scale, those numbers were heavily shaped by seasonality, quarter cycles, and external environment. Chasing them directly felt like chasing noise.</p>

  <div className="pull">
    <p>The better question was not “how do we get more users?” but “how do we move existing users inward, from basic usage toward deeper dependency?”</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="section">
  <div className="section-label">Two Findings</div>
  <h2>The opportunity sat at the intersection of mobile intent and mid-level capability.</h2>

  <div className="problem-grid">
    <div className="problem-cell">
      <span className="p-icon">📱</span>
      <h3>Mobile was active, but badly matched to user intent</h3>
      <p>A large share of active usage came from mobile, yet the interface was overloaded and designed as if users were building spreadsheets on small screens. In reality, they were mostly viewing, scanning, and navigating.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="problem-cell">
      <span className="p-icon">📈</span>
      <h3>Mid-level features had value, but the barrier was too high</h3>
      <p>Features like conditional formatting were strategically useful in real business scenarios, but penetration stayed low because the product asked too much learning up front.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>
