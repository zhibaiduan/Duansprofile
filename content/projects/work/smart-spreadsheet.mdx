That also meant its direction was still undefined, and defining that direction was my real job.</p>

  <p className="prose">The company already had a traditional spreadsheet and a multi-dimensional table product. If the smart spreadsheet was just another tool in between, it would be redundant. The work started with strategy before it ever became roadmap.</p>

<div className="section">
  <div className="section-label">My Thinking</div>
  <h2>The wedge was not “better spreadsheet.” It was “system-building without forcing a new mental model.”</h2>

  <p className="prose">The spreadsheet market is crowded, and competing on basic capabilities creates no moat. Research showed that roughly one in five usage scenarios involved people building internal business systems inside spreadsheets. These users had long-lived files, multi-person collaboration, and high rebuild costs.</p>

  <div className="pull">
    <p>The spreadsheet is the original prototype of both SaaS software and low-code tools. Users were already trying to build systems. The product simply was not designed to help them do it well.</p>
  </div>

  <div className="journey">
    <div className="journey-step">
      <div className="step-dot">1</div>
      <div className="step-body">
        <h4>Traditional spreadsheets are familiar, but fragile at scale</h4>
        <p>Most users hit a ceiling as soon as workflows become more collaborative, more structured, or more operationally important.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <div className="journey-step">
      <div className="step-dot">2</div>
      <div className="step-body">
        <h4>Database-style products solve some of this, but adoption is expensive</h4>
        <p>Users often need to abandon the spreadsheet mental model they already understand, which creates learning friction and slows migration.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <div className="journey-step">
      <div className="step-dot">3</div>
      <div className="step-body">
        <h4>The opportunity was to guide, not replace</h4>
        <p>Start from the familiar spreadsheet surface, then help users move toward more structured, dependable system-building over time.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="section">
  <div className="section-label">Positioning</div>
  <h2>More capable than a spreadsheet. Less demanding than Airtable.</h2>

  <div className="problem-grid">
    <div className="problem-cell">
      <span className="p-icon">🧩</span>
      <h3>What it should preserve</h3>
      <p>The familiarity, flexibility, and low cognitive barrier that make spreadsheets so widely adopted in the first place.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="problem-cell">
      <span className="p-icon">🛠️</span>
      <h3>What it should add</h3>
      <p>Structure, safer collaboration, mobile readiness, and the ability to turn a file into a lightweight internal system.</p>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<div className="section">
  <div className="section-label">Example Scenario</div>
  <h2>A spreadsheet becomes a self-service internal app without changing the data underneath.</h2>

  <p className="prose">A simple HR spreadsheet can become an employee information service without migrating the data source or forcing users into a totally different product model.</p>

  <div className="problem-grid">
    <div className="problem-cell">
      <span className="p-icon">📄</span>
      <h3>Before</h3>
      <p>Employees message HR manually. HR replies with screenshots or ad hoc exports. Permissions are hard to control because the whole file is exposed.</p>
    </div>
    <div className="problem-cell">
      <span className="p-icon">📱</span>
      <h3>After</h3>
      <p>HR builds a lightweight query app on top of the spreadsheet. Employees only see their own row-level data. The flow becomes mobile-friendly and self-service by default.</p>
    </div>
